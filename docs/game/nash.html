The Formal Definition

A strategy is strictly dominant if, for any opponent strategy \(s_{-i}\):

\[ u_i(s_i', s_{-i}) > u_i(s_i'', s_{-i}) \]

Where: \(u_i\) = your payoff, \(s_i'\) = dominant strategy, \(s_i''\) = other strategy, \(s_{-i}\) = opponent's choice

(Your payoff from the dominant move is strictly GREATER than from any other move.)

Here's the formal math.

A set of strategies is a Nash Equilibrium if:

(No player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.)

Mathematical Foundation

A mixed strategy \(\sigma_i\) for you as player \(i\) assigns probability \(\sigma_i(s_i)\) to each pure strategy \(s_i\), where:

\[ \sum_{s_i \in S_i} \sigma_i(s_i) = 1 \text{ and } \sigma_i(s_i) \geq 0 \quad \forall s_i \]

In plain English: You as player \(i\) assign a probability to each of your possible moves, and these probabilities must add up to 100% (you have to do something), and none can be negative.

Expected Utility Computation

When players use mixed strategies, your expected utility as player \(i\) is \(u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i})\):

Where you as player \(i\) have strategy \(\sigma_i\), opponents have strategies \(\sigma_{-i}\)

\[ u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i}) = \sum_{s \in S} u_i(s) \prod_{j \in N} \sigma_j(s_j) \]

In plain English: We sum \(\sum\) over all possible outcomes \(s\). For each outcome, we multiply \(\prod\) YOUR payoff \(u_i(s)\) by the probability that outcome happens. The probability is the product of each player \(j\)'s chance \(\sigma_j(s_j)\) of choosing their part of that outcome.

The Mathematical Secret: Indifference Condition

In a Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium, you as player \(i\) must be indifferent between all strategies you play with positive probability:

Where you as player \(i\) have different strategies \(s_i\) and \(s_i'\), opponents have strategies \(\sigma_{-i}\)

\[ u_i(s_i, \sigma_{-i}) = u_i(s_i', \sigma_{-i}) \quad \forall s_i, s_i' \text{ with } \sigma_i(s_i), \sigma_i(s_i') > 0 \]

In plain English: YOU must be equally happy with each of YOUR moves \(s_i\) and \(s_i'\). If you preferred one move over another, you'd just always choose the better one instead of randomizing.

Kicker: Left 0.3 1.0

Let q = probability dive Left. Make Kicker indifferent:

\[ u_K(\text{Kick Left}, \sigma_G) = u_K(\text{Kick Right}, \sigma_G) \]

\[ 0.3q + 1.0(1-q) = 0.9q + 0(1-q) \]

Solve: 0.3q + 1 - q = 0.9q → 1 = 1.6q → q = 0.625
