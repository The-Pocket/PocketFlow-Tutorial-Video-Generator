The Bernoulli Distribution

The Atom of Decision

This is the Bernoulli distribution.

A single experiment with two outcomes, defined by one parameter, p (the probability of success).

Outcome Coded As (k) Probability
Success 1 \(P(X=1) = p\)
Failure 0 \(P(X=0) = 1-p\)

Mathematicians cooked up this single formula, the Probability Mass Function (PMF), to express both lines at once:

\[ P(X=k) = p^k(1-p)^{1-k}, \quad \text{for } k \in \{0,1\} \]

This looks way more complex than it is. It's just a clever trick.

Let's prove it's just a machine that spits out the right probability.

\[ P(X=k) = p^k(1-p)^{1-k}, \quad \text{for } k \in \{0,1\} \]

1. What's the probability of Success (k=1)?

\( P(X=1) = p^1(1-p)^{1-1} \)

\( = p \cdot (1-p)^0 \)

\( = p \cdot 1 = \) p. Correct.

2. What's the probability of Failure (k=0)?

\( P(X=0) = p^0(1-p)^{1-0} \)

\( = 1 \cdot (1-p)^1 \)

\( = \) 1-p. Correct.

"Getting k successes in n trials"?

That's the Binomial Distribution!

It's a problem you've already solved!

We just plug our new numbers into the Binomial formula:

Binomial: \(P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\)

Our Setup: \(n = k-1\) and \(k = r-1\)

\[ \binom{k-1}{r-1} p^{r-1} (1-p)^{(k-1)-(r-1)} \] \[ \implies \binom{k-1}{r-1} p^{r-1} (1-p)^{k-r} \]

Now, just combine the "setup" with the "grand finale."

\[ P(X=k) = \underbrace{\binom{k-1}{r-1} p^{r-1} (1-p)^{k-r}}_{\text{The Binomial setup for r-1 successes in k-1 trials}} \times \underbrace{p}_{\text{The final winning success}} \]

Combine the \(p\) terms, and you get the full formula. It was just a Binomial problem in disguise.

See? Not so scary.

Problem: Free Throws

A player makes 80% \(p=0.8\) of free throws. What's the probability she makes her 3rd basket \(r=3\) on her 4th attempt \(k=4\)?

Problem: Free Throws

A player makes 80% \(p=0.8\) of free throws. She wants her 3rd basket \(r=3\) on her 4th attempt \(k=4\).

1. The final shot (shot #4) must be a make. Probability = 0.8

2. In the first 3 shots, she must have made exactly 2. (Binomial)

Binomial part: \(P(2 \text{ in } 3) = \binom{3}{2}(0.8)^2(0.2)^1 = 0.384\)

Total Probability = (Binomial Part) × (Final Shot)

\(0.384 \times 0.8 = 0.3072\)

30.72% chance

The Intuitive Mean

The expected waiting time is just as elegant:

\[ \mu = \frac{r}{p} \]

If you wait \(1/p\) for one success (Geometric), you expect to wait \(r\) times longer for \(r\) successes.

Example: Sales Quota

A salesperson has a 10% close rate \(p=0.1\) and a quota of 5 sales \(r=5\).

Their expected number of calls to hit that quota is:

\[ \mu = \frac{5}{0.1} = \mathbf{50} \]

50 calls. It makes perfect sense.

The Origin Story: Where does Poisson's formula come from?

You've seen this formula. It looks like it came from another planet.

\[P(X=k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}\]

Where did that \(e\) come from? Why is there a factorial?

It's not magic.

It's the ghost of the Binomial distribution.

We're going to derive it in 60 seconds.

Deriving Poisson from Binomial

Start with the Binomial PMF and substitute \(p = \lambda / n\).

Step 1: Start with the Binomial Formula

\[ P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \]
\[ \implies \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^k \left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-k} \]

Deriving Poisson from Binomial

As \(n \to \infty\), simplify the terms.

Step 2: Simplify the "Success" Part (rearrange the terms)

\[ P(X=k) = \frac{n(n-1)...(n-k+1)}{n^k} \times \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} \times \left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-k} \]

Deriving Poisson from Binomial

As \(n \to \infty\), simplify the terms.

As \(n\) becomes infinite, the first term just becomes 1.

\[ P(X=k) = \color{#ff6b6b}{1} \times \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} \times \left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-k} \]

We're left with: \( \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} \)

Deriving Poisson from Binomial

As \(n \to \infty\), simplify the terms.

Step 3: Simplify the "Failure" Part

The last piece is \( \left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-k} \). Since \(n\) is HUGE, we can ignore the \(-k\).

\[ \implies \left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n} \]

Does that look familiar?

This is one of the fundamental definitions from calculus:

\[ \lim_{n\to\infty} \left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n = e^x \]

In our case, \(x = -\lambda\).

So our entire failure term just becomes \(e^{-\lambda}\).

The Grand Finale

Put the two pieces together.

Success Part:
\( \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} \)
Failure Part:
\( e^{-\lambda} \)
\[ P(X=k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} \]

It's not arbitrary.

It's the mathematical destination of counting successes when the trials become infinite and the events become rare.

How to Use It

The Poisson distribution is defined by a single parameter:

λ (lambda): The average number of events in a given interval.

It answers the question: "If the average is \(\lambda\), what's the probability of observing exactly \(k\) events?"

Problem: Support Center Calls

A support center receives an average of 5 calls per hour \(\lambda=5\).

What's the probability they receive exactly 2 calls \(k=2\) in the next hour?

Problem: Support Center Calls

A support center receives an average of 5 calls per hour \(\lambda=5\).

Solution: Plug it into the formula.

\[ P(X=2) = \frac{5^2 \times e^{-5}}{2!} \] \[ P(X=2) = \frac{25 \times 0.00674}{2} \]
\[ P(X=2) \approx 0.084 \text{ or } \boxed{8.4\%} \]

The Golden Rule of Poisson:

Your \(\lambda\) MUST match your interval.

If you want to find the probability of getting 1 call in the next 30 minutes \(0.5 \text{ hours}\), you must scale your rate:

New \(\lambda\) = \(5 \text{ calls/hour}\) × \(0.5 \text{ hours}\)

= 2.5

Here's the most important practical takeaway.

For a Poisson process:

Mean: \(\mu = \lambda\)

Variance: \(\sigma^2 = \lambda\)

The mean is EQUAL to the variance.

This is the signature of a Poisson process.

If you're looking at real-world count data and the mean and variance are roughly the same...

...you should immediately think Poisson.

It's your diagnostic tool.

The Brilliant Insight

Let's ask: What is the probability that the waiting time \(X\) for the next event is *longer* than some amount of time \(x\)?

It means there were ZERO events.

So, these two questions are the exact same thing:

"What's the probability the waiting time is longer than \(x\)?"

"What's the probability of having zero events in an interval of length \(x\)?"

And we have a tool for the second question!

It's the Poisson formula we just learned!

Deriving Exponential from Poisson

We want the probability of zero events \(k=0\) in an interval of length \(x\).

1. Start with the Poisson PMF:

\[P(X=k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}\]

Deriving Exponential from Poisson

We want the probability of zero events \(k=0\) in an interval of length \(x\).

2. Set up the "Zero Events" problem:

  • The new average for our interval is \(\lambda x\).
  • We want to find the probability of \(k=0\) events.

Deriving Exponential from Poisson

We want the probability of zero events \(k=0\) in an interval of length \(x\).

3. Plug into the Poisson Formula:

\[ P(k=0) = \frac{(\lambda x)^0 \times e^{-\lambda x}}{0!} \]
\[ P(k=0) = \frac{1 \times e^{-\lambda x}}{1} = e^{-\lambda x} \]

And because the two questions were the same...

\[ P(X > x) = e^{-\lambda x} \]

This tells us the probability of waiting longer than \(x\).

But we usually want the probability of waiting less than \(x\). That's the CDF.

\[ F(x) = P(X \le x) = 1 - P(X > x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x} \]

And if you want the PDF, you just take the derivative of the CDF.

\[ f(x) = \frac{d}{dx}(1 - e^{-\lambda x}) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \]

See? It's not magic.

The Exponential distribution is born directly from the logic of the Poisson.

Problem: Server Failure

A critical server fails at an average rate of \(\lambda = 0.5\) times per year.

What is the probability that it fails within the first 6 months \(0.5 \text{ years}\)?

Problem: Server Failure

A critical server fails at an average rate of \(\lambda = 0.5\) times per year.

Solution: We want \(P(X \leq 0.5)\), a job for the CDF.

\[ F(0.5) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x} \] \[ F(0.5) = 1 - e^{-(0.5)(0.5)} = 1 - e^{-0.25} \] \[ F(0.5) \approx 1 - 0.7788 = 0.2212 \]

There is a 22.1% chance the server fails.

And now for the final, beautiful connection.

The expected waiting time, or Mean:

\[ \mu = \frac{1}{\lambda} \]

This is perfect. It's the inverse relationship we'd expect.

The faster the rate, the shorter the wait.

If the rate of calls \(\lambda\) is 5 per hour...

...then the average time between calls \(\mu\) is 1/5 of an hour \(12 \text{ minutes}\).
